Internet and Its Effect on Our Lives

The internet has been booming since 1969. By the mid-1990s, it became publicly accessible and quickly grew into the main road for information, education, entertainment and culture.

1969 The internet journey began.
1995 Around 16 million global users.
2000 361 million users, about 6% of the population.
2015 The user base reached 3 billion.
2025 5.6 billion users, around 68% of the population.

Monopoly on the Internet

Over the last decade, digital platforms have shifted from open access to layered monetization. What used to be a single subscription now often includes rental fees, region locks and add-on purchases.


For example, a user may pay $12 for platform access and still be asked to pay $6 more to watch a new release. Add $15 for an academic e-book and $5 for news access, and the total rises quickly. For many students and families, this isn’t sustainable — it forces hard choices about what knowledge or entertainment is “worth” paying for.

The Real Access Gap

When legal access becomes expensive, fragmented and inconvenient, people look for alternatives. The issue isn’t just price — it’s complexity. Multiple apps, multiple payments, region restrictions and scattered resources create friction that pushes users away from legitimate ecosystems.

Why Only KryptosAbyss?

KryptosAbyss provides a new dimension to making culture accessible for all. The project focuses on a smoother, safer and more user-friendly way to search for culture, knowledge and digital resources.

1

Accessible

Instead of navigating five different subscriptions, users can discover available resources through a clearer and more organized pathway.

2

Safer

By guiding users toward structured and legitimate sources, KryptosAbyss reduces the risks associated with unsafe or misleading websites.

3

Simple

A clean interface focused on search and discovery — no unnecessary clutter, no endless redirects.

4

Cultural

The goal is long-term: enabling wider participation in film, literature, journalism,entertainment and educational content.

“Access to culture should depend on curiosity,not on how many subscriptions you can afford.”
